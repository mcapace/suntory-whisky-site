WHAT MAKES A WHISKY TRULY JAPANESE

THE QUIET ASCENT OF JAPANESE WHISKY

With global demand came global confusion—blurring the lines between what's truly made in Japan and what merely bears the name. As acclaim grew, so did imitation as misleading labels entered the market.

Suntory's Leadership and the New Standards

To protect the future, Suntory helped establish the Japanese Spirits & Liqueurs Makers Association (JSLMA) standards in 2021. These define authentic Japanese whisky: made entirely in Japan from malted grains, aged for at least three years, and bottled at a minimum 40% ABV.

Authenticity is not origin alone—it is process, philosophy, and purpose. The House of Suntory is built on three pillars of Japanese culture: Wa, harmony with Japanese people, nature and culture; Monozukuri, the tradition of Japanese craftsmanship; and Omotenashi, the sharing of authentic Japanese experiences.

THE RISE OF JAPANESE BARTENDING

The cocktail may have been born in America, but its evolution has found new depth behind Japanese bars—and now, across the world. Since the early days of the cocktail revival, Japanese bartending has captured the imagination of American bartenders and patrons alike.

Japanese bartending, long admired for its precision and detail, has become a point of connection and creative exchange among bartenders around the world. This cultural allure mirrors a broader American obsession with Japanese aesthetics—from the tea ceremony to the skills of the itamae or master sushi chef—where discipline becomes art, and every gesture matters.

Today, these shared values are showing up across cocktail culture—from Tokyo to New York—where hospitality is treated as an art form. At the heart of this movement is a deep respect for beauty, precision, and the kind of craftsmanship long championed by The House of Suntory.

"Japanese bartending is like a tea ceremony: quiet, precise, and deeply personal. From carving ice to stirring a highball, every gesture is intentional—crafted to honor one guest in one perfect moment."

— Shintaro Eleazar Tozzo, Bar Moga, New York
